The Homeless Population and COVID-19

Homeless Men vs Women In Shelter

According to NYC OpenData , from the period between 2014 to 2022, the amount of men and women residing in homeless shelters has steadily been increasing. However, during the time at which COVID-19 had begun, around 2019, the rate at which homeless men had been admitted to homeless shelters had sharply increased. What's interesting to note is that compared to homeless male admisisons to homeless shelters, the rate at which females had been admitted to homeless shelters is drastically lower. In fact, we can see that from the period of 2019-2020, the number of homeless women admitted to homeless shelters decreased! This is certainly a strange phenomenon. However, this could be due to the fact that the number of homeless men far exceeds the number of homeless women, so although the rates are different, the rate at which homeless women are admitted to homeless shelters is affected more as more data is added to the pool. Another observation to note is that from 2021 to 2022, there is a large drop in the number of homeless men admitted to homeless shelters. Now, although this may not mean that the number of homeless people dropped in general, there certainly is the idea that homeless relief programs have been made to lower the number of homeless people admitted to these shelters, implying that their lives have significantly grown better in terms of quality. In regards to the regression lines for both homeless men and women admitted to homeless shelters, the slope for homeless men is much steeper than that of homeless women. This implies that the rate at which homeless men that were admitted at the time of COVID-19 was much higher than that of women. This may be due to many factors, but the main ones to note are the job layoffs for male-dominated jobs, such as construction, office jobs, business, etc. The large dip in number of homeless men admitted to shelters in 2021-2022 helped to lessen this slope, but nonetheless it is still absurdly high. The same goes for homeless women in shelters. Although the numbers are significantly less, the regression line tells us that it predicts to continue rising.

Job Loss Rate In Different States During COVID-19

This chart presents the job loss rate of all 50 states in the United States. As presented by the data, 48 states have had an increase in job loss rates, as is to be expected. What is interesting to see is the fact that 2 states, Idaho and Utah, had a negative job loss rate. This means that there was an increase in the number of jobs given during the COVID-19 pandemic! This may be due to the fact that COVID-19 had different impacts in each state, and in places where COVID-19 had a small effect on job loss rates, jobs relating to the medical field were most likely given to many in efforts to suppress COVID-19. Jobs such as test-takers, hospital staff, and more were most likely given to these individuals, and as a result, the number of jobs increased. In regards to the other 48 states, its clear to see that the pandemic had a negative effect on jobs, as many people were unfortunately laid off, and even fired. It is interesting to see that Hawaii had the highest job loss rate during COVID-19, as it is a state far off from the main-land. However, it is also important to note that the job-loss rates are directly correlated to the population of each state, as the number of jobs are affected by the number of people. This means that states with smaller populations that were affected by COVID-19 negatively were susceptible to higher job loss rates. So, seeing states such as New York, with high populations and a high job loss rate, really displays how devastating COVID-19 was on the economy and peoples lives. According to the bar-graph, there are two peaks in job loss rates, one in Hawaii, and one in New York. This tells us that the mode of the job loss rates exists in these states.

Homeless People Encountered in Hospitals

The graph on the right displays the trend in homeless people being admitted to hospitals during the period from 2017 - 2021. This information is important to present because it displays how COVID-19 affected the access to healthcare centers for homeless people. As you can see from the graph, from the period between 2017-2019, there was a general increase in the number of homeless people encountered in hospitals. However, as the pandemic begun, this number drastically decreased. This may be due to a number of reasons. One reason can be the prioritization of non-homeless people, who show that they have insurance or a positive record. Another reason can be homeless people know that they can't afford hospitas, so they don't want to put themselves in more financial burder. A third reason can be simply because many homeless people have unfortunately passed away from the pandemic, and have not received the proper help that they needed. However, towards the end of 2020, as COVID-19 began to die down, the number of homeless people admitted into hospitals drastically increased again. This can be due to medical reasons besides COVID-19 of course, but nonetheless efforts to help homeless people have sprung up again and have led to this drastic increase. The map displayed on the top right of this page displays areas in the United States aimed at helping homeless people that have emerged during the COVID-19 pandemic. This shows that efforts helping homeless people out during the pandemic have significantly increased, and as a result the number of homeless people receiving services such as healthcare have also gone up.
